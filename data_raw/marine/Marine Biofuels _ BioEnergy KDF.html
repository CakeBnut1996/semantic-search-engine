Marine Biofuels

Annually, the global merchant marine fleet uses over 300 million metric tonnes of heavy petroleum fuel oils to transport over 80% of all goods shipped worldwide. This demand represents a significant opportunity for the United States to expand its domestic fuel production capabilities. 

Given the robust, high-capacity engines on large ocean-going vessels, and the unique ability of large two-stroke marine engines to operate effectively on less stringent residual oils, the maritime sector is uniquely positioned to take advantage of minimally-processed biofuels from underutilized domestic sources (such as biomass, waste materials, and bio-intermediates) as a new, cost-effective fuel pathway to support American energy security and supply chain resilience. 

To determine the efficacy of biofuels as a replacement for marine petroleum fuel oils, research is needed to determine pathway cost reductions, scalability, and compatibility with existing marine engines, fuel storage, and distribution systems.

Marine Biofuel Research at the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE)

The U.S. Department of Energy (DOE), through its Bioenergy Technologies Office (BETO), is funding research at the Argonne, Oak Ridge, Pacific Northwest, and the National Renewable Energy National Laboratories to assess how biomass-derived marine fuels can be produced, distributed, and used at scale in the maritime sector. These efforts focus on identifying feedstock conversion technologies—including biochemical, thermochemical, electrochemical, and hybrid approaches—that can affordably deliver alternative marine fuels compatible with existing ship engines and fuel infrastructure. By harnessing a variety of domestic feedstocks—from energy crops and algae to municipal solid waste and industrial byproducts—DOE aims to reduce reliance on imported fuels, create new industrial supply chains, and generate economic opportunities in rural and industrial communities across the nation.

The national laboratories are working in close partnership with the U.S. Department of Transportation’s Maritime Administration and key industry stakeholders to understand the technical requirements, logistics, pricing structures, and market dynamics of alternative fuels for the maritime sector. The work reflects the United States’ strategic interest in maintaining a secure, affordable, and reliable energy base for critical transportation sectors — ensuring American competitiveness and supply chain leadership in global shipping.
